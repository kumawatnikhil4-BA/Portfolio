Real-World Scenario · Energy SaaS

Onboarding a new European intraday power vendor , under a regulatory deadline, with a broken sample file.

Advanced

01 , The Situation

A new ticket lands in Jira: a European power exchange needs their 15-minute intraday power prices for Germany, France, and Austria integrated into MarketView. The client behind this request has a contractual go-live date tied to SDAC.

The vendor's sample file arrives with three problems: inconsistent field headers between markets, German data in CET while French data is in UTC, and Austrian prices labelled MW when the values are actually MWh.

02 , What I Did First: Nothing Gets Specced Yet

The instinct of a junior analyst is to start mapping fields immediately. My first move is the opposite , I map what I don't know. I read the sample file three times and list every inconsistency, then calculate the downstream risk of each one.

If I assume CET throughout and the French source is genuinely UTC, every French price ships one hour offset. For an intraday power product used in live trading decisions, that isn't a data quality issue , it's a liability with real financial consequence.

03 , Structured Vendor Communication

I sent one precise email to the vendor's data team , every ambiguity grouped by category (fields, timezone, units, schedule), referenced to the exact line in their sample file, with the business consequence of each explained.

Result: Full clarification received in 48 hours , vendors respond faster when they understand why a detail matters, not just that it's missing.

04 , Symbol Pattern Design

While waiting on the vendor, I designed the symbol pattern , not a data-entry task, an architectural one. Every client using this data, every filter in platform search, and every future European vendor added after this one inherits this decision.

Pattern chosen: NGRD_{MKT}_{FREQ}_{CCY}_{UNIT} → e.g. NGRD_DE_15M_EUR_MWH. Vendor prefix avoids collision with existing sources. Frequency is explicit, never implied. Unit is embedded directly because the vendor's own file had a unit-labelling error.

05 , Writing the Specification

The spec had to be buildable by a developer who has never spoken to the vendor and doesn't know what intraday power is. It covered: symbol pattern and variants, plain-English descriptions, full source-to-target field mapping, timezone conversion logic, missing-data rules, validation thresholds, and edge cases.

  • DecisionMissing 15-min buckets stored as NULL, never zero , zero is a valid power price in some markets; NULL signals true absence.
  • DecisionAll timestamps converted to UTC at ingest, display timezone stored separately , resolves the CET/UTC ambiguity permanently, at source.
  • DecisionAustrian unit corrected to MWh in the spec, with a flagged note on the vendor's header error , so the next analyst knows it's intentional, not a typo.

    • 06 , Sprint & Delivery

    Jira story written with acceptance criteria mirroring the spec's validation rules. Developer briefed in 10 minutes, not an hour , because the spec left nothing open. Source connection tested by me before development began. Confluence updated for reuse.

    Why I Bother With All This

    Honestly, most of this could be skipped if I was in a hurry. But a wrong assumption here doesn't just break a dashboard, someone downstream is trading on that number. I've written this same level of detail whether it was my 1st integration or my 200th.
